## Executive View

VerityAir Systems Ltd is entering a market that is already real and growing, but still fragmented through its platform, VerityATLAS.

The key point is this:

there are many drone tools, but relatively fewer products that connect mission planning, readiness, live operational context, and audit evidence into one assurance-led operating layer.

That means VerityATLAS does not sit cleanly inside only one software category.

It overlaps:

- drone operations management
- compliance and readiness workflows
- airspace and flight planning tools
- replay and operational review
- BVLOS enablement and future UTM-adjacent workflows

## Market Size

### 1. Broad Commercial Drone Market

The broad commercial drone market is already large and still expanding.

Key current reference points:

- Grand View Research estimates the global commercial drone market at **USD 30.02 billion in 2024** and **USD 54.64 billion by 2030**.
- Grand View Research estimates the Europe commercial drone market at **USD 7.63 billion in 2024** and **USD 12.96 billion by 2030**.
- Grand View Research estimates the UK commercial drone market at **USD 980.5 million in 2024** and **USD 1.50 billion by 2030**.

These figures are useful as category context, but they are broader than VerityATLAS's direct software opportunity because they include hardware and wider commercial drone spend.

### 2. Broad Drone Software Market

This is a better top-level market reference for VerityATLAS.

Key current reference points:

- Grand View Research estimates the global drone software market at **USD 9.27 billion in 2024** and **USD 24.39 billion by 2030**.
- Grand View Research estimates the **commercial** drone software segment at **USD 4.89 billion in 2024** and **USD 12.72 billion by 2030**.

This is the most useful broad software TAM reference for VerityATLAS.

### 3. UK and Regulatory Growth Signals

Official UK and U.S. sources support the growth story even where they do not give identical market-revenue numbers.

- The UK government says commercial drones could be worth **GBP 45 billion to the UK economy by 2030**. That is economic impact, not software spend, so it should not be presented as VerityATLAS's TAM.
- The UK CAA said on **21 October 2025** that its roadmap sets a path to **routine BVLOS operations in the UK by 2027**.
- The FAA forecast says the U.S. commercial drone fleet totaled about **966,000 aircraft at the end of 2024** and is forecast to reach about **1.18 million by 2029**.

Together, these support the claim that:

- the installed base is growing
- operational complexity is rising
- the need for structured assurance, compliance, and operational control should increase with BVLOS adoption

## What Market VerityATLAS Should Actually Target

### Practical Positioning

VerityAir Systems Ltd should not pitch VerityATLAS as serving the entire drone market.

That would be too broad and too vague.

The best investor framing is:

VerityAir Systems Ltd is targeting the enterprise mission-assurance software layer inside regulated commercial drone operations with VerityATLAS, especially operators already working under an Operational Authorisation, insurance constraints, and accountable readiness burden.

### Suggested Market Layers

#### Broad TAM

Global commercial drone software:

- **USD 4.89 billion in 2024**
- **USD 12.72 billion by 2030**

#### Practical SAM

This is an inference, not a published market number.

If we treat VerityATLAS's beachhead as the operations-management, compliance, assurance, and audit slice of commercial drone software, a reasonable working assumption is that this category is a meaningful subset of the total software market rather than the whole of it.

For investor modelling, a cautious planning band is:

- roughly **10% to 20%** of the commercial drone software market

That implies a rough global beachhead software market of:

- about **USD 0.5 billion to USD 1.0 billion** in 2024
- about **USD 1.3 billion to USD 2.5 billion** by 2030

This should be presented as a reasoned estimate, not as a third-party market statistic.

#### Europe And UK Proxy

These are also inferences based on regional share of the broader commercial drone market.

If Europe keeps a similar share of the relevant software opportunity, the broad commercial drone software opportunity proxy for Europe is roughly:

- about **USD 1.2 billion** in 2024
- about **USD 3.2 billion** by 2030

If the UK keeps a similar share, the broad commercial drone software opportunity proxy for the UK is roughly:

- about **USD 0.16 billion** in 2024
- about **USD 0.42 billion** by 2030

Applying the same 10% to 20% assurance-layer assumption gives a rough UK beachhead range of:

- about **USD 16 million to USD 32 million** in 2024
- about **USD 42 million to USD 84 million** by 2030

These UK numbers are small enough to force discipline and large enough to support a specialist company if the product genuinely becomes the system of record for regulated operators.

## Competitor Landscape

## Category 1: Enterprise Drone Operations Management

These are the closest commercial software competitors in day-to-day buyer conversations.

### Aloft

Position:

- integrated fleet and airspace management
- strong U.S. compliance and authorization orientation
- enterprise workflows, flight logs, checklists, risk assessments, and LAANC

Evidence:

- Aloft describes itself as the **"#1 FAA Approved Airspace Platform"** with **"Integrated Fleet & Airspace Management"**
- it says it offers a single platform for airspace management, fleet management, flight logging, checklists, risk assessments, and LAANC

Why it matters:

- strong benchmark for operational workflow and compliance in the U.S.
- especially relevant if VerityATLAS expands toward approval workflows and operational system-of-record positioning

Gap versus VerityATLAS:

- stronger current U.S. airspace authorization footprint
- VerityATLAS appears stronger in assurance-chain thinking, audit evidence structure, and mission readiness framing

### FlyFreely

Position:

- drone mission planning, compliance, and fleet management
- aimed at site and asset owners
- operational efficiency and compliance platform

Evidence:

- FlyFreely says it offers **"Drone mission planning, compliance & fleet management software"**

Why it matters:

- very close to VerityATLAS's practical buyer story
- especially relevant for enterprise infrastructure and industrial operators

Gap versus VerityATLAS:

- close adjacency on mission planning and compliance
- VerityATLAS's distinctive angle is stronger end-to-end assurance continuity and audit logic if developed clearly

### DroneDeploy Operations Management

Position:

- broad enterprise drone operations platform
- combines operational management, compliance, pilot and equipment administration, and live views
- heavily tied to data capture and enterprise site workflows

Evidence:

- DroneDeploy says it offers operations management to automate and centralize **flight, operations, and compliance**
- it highlights custom pre-flight checklists, pilot credentials, equipment management, and live streaming

Why it matters:

- large commercial presence and strong enterprise credibility
- could overlap in pilot/equipment/compliance workflow conversations

Gap versus VerityATLAS:

- stronger today in scale, capture workflows, and enterprise brand recognition
- VerityATLAS can differentiate around aviation-style assurance, decision evidence, readiness gates, and auditability

### Airspace Link AirHub Portal

Position:

- centralized drone operations management
- strong U.S. regulatory integration, B4UFLY, LAANC, risk assessment, and operational visibility
- increasingly aimed at government and commercial fleets

Evidence:

- Airspace Link describes AirHub as an **FAA-Approved Drone Operations Management System**
- it highlights real-time airspace awareness, streamlined risk assessments, built-in B4UFLY and LAANC, full regulatory compliance, and complete audit trails

Why it matters:

- relevant competitor for regulated operations, particularly where airspace and public-sector workflows matter

Gap versus VerityATLAS:

- stronger U.S. regulatory-data integration
- VerityATLAS can be stronger if it keeps building readiness, audit evidence, replay, and mission-assurance continuity

## What This Means For VerityATLAS

## Competitor Scale Signals

Public competitor scale claims are uneven.

Some companies publish clear user, customer, or adoption numbers. Others rely more on deployments, partners, or workflow statistics than on direct customer counts.

That means investor discussion should distinguish between:

- direct customer-count claims
- user or pilot-count claims
- site, flight, or agency-count claims
- deployment footprint claims

### Aloft

Public signal:

- Aloft says it has earned the trust of **over 100,000 customers** and **2 million flights**

What this means:

- one of the clearest public customer-scale claims in the comparison set
- especially important as a signal of broad workflow adoption in commercial drone operations

### DroneDeploy

Public signal:

- DroneDeploy says it is used on **over 3 million sites** in **180 countries**

What this means:

- strong evidence of large operational footprint
- but this is a site-count signal, not a direct customer-count signal

### AirData UAV

Public signal:

- AirData shows **452,897 active pilots**
- **61,754,666 flights uploaded**
- **232 countries**
- its About page says it helps **tens of thousands** of drone fleet operators

What this means:

- strong proof of broad usage and telemetry scale
- more of a usage-scale and pilot-scale signal than a clean published enterprise-customer count

### Airspace Link / AirHub

Public signal:

- Airspace Link shows **89,145 monthly active users**
- **6,343 unique government and commercial agencies**

What this means:

- one of the better public adoption signals in the market
- especially useful because it points to organisational adoption, not just flight or app activity

### Unifly

Public signal:

- technology deployed in **more than 10 countries**
- **over 5 million flights**
- **8 live commercial deployments**
- **10 global UTM platforms**

What this means:

- strong deployment and infrastructure signal
- stronger as a UTM maturity indicator than as a direct published customer-count claim

### OneSky

Public signal:

- **50,000 users worldwide**
- **700 aerospace organizations**

What this means:

- useful adoption-scale signal
- especially relevant because it reflects aerospace and authority-grade software heritage as well as UTM positioning

### FlyFreely

Public signal:

- no clear public customer-count claim found
- softer claims include being trusted by organisations of different sizes and pricing built around remote-pilot licence volume

What this means:

- market presence appears credible, but public scale transparency is limited
- buyers are likely to rely more on demos, references, and direct proof than on headline customer numbers

### SkyGrid

Public signal:

- no clear public customer-count claim found
- public material emphasizes partners, platform vision, and Boeing or Wisk alignment

What this means:

- strong strategic backing signal
- weak public transparency on actual customer or deployment count

### Droniq

Public signal:

- no clear public customer-count claim found
- public material highlights operational proof signals such as **more than 12,000 km BVLOS** flown

What this means:

- credible operational and services signal
- weaker public evidence on customer-count scale

### Summary Read-Through

The strongest public scale signals currently visible are:

- **Aloft** for direct customer-count language
- **Airspace Link** for agency and active-user adoption
- **AirData** for pilot and flight-volume scale
- **DroneDeploy** for site footprint
- **Unifly** for deployment maturity
- **OneSky** for user and organisation count

This matters for VerityATLAS because it clarifies who looks large, but it also shows that much of the market still lacks clean, universally comparable customer transparency.

That helps the VerityATLAS story if positioned correctly:

- some competitors have scale
- many do not clearly own the assurance-led category
- public size signals do not remove the opportunity for a focused specialist platform to win a regulated operator niche first

## Honest Positioning

VerityATLAS does not appear to be strongest where:

- the product needs national-scale UTM infrastructure
- regulatory authorization is directly integrated with aviation authorities
- the product is sold primarily as commodity flight logging or mapping

VerityATLAS appears strongest where:

- operators need a single assurance record across mission lifecycle stages
- mission readiness depends on pilot, platform, airspace, and risk checks together
- post-operation evidence and accountability matter
- enterprise buyers want aviation-style operational discipline rather than only drone-app convenience

## Suggested Position In The Market

Best current positioning:

VerityATLAS is a mission assurance and auditability platform for regulated drone operations.

Best future positioning:

VerityATLAS becomes the assurance layer that sits above mission planning, airspace awareness, and BVLOS-enablement services.

That is important because it means VerityATLAS does not need to beat every UTM provider at being a UTM.

It needs to become the system that makes the operator safe, explainable, and investment-grade.

### Today

We are targeting the enterprise assurance layer inside commercial drone software.

### Near-Term Expansion

We expand deeper into regulated operator workflows:

- mission approval
- dispatch
- live operational context
- replay
- audit evidence

### Longer-Term Expansion

We expand toward:

- BVLOS assurance
- multi-authority regulatory profiles
- operator-center workflows
- selective UTM and U-Space integrations
- dual-use and critical-infrastructure assurance applications

## Bottom Line

The market is large enough to support a serious company.

The broad software TAM is attractive, but VerityATLAS should win by dominating a narrower and more defensible segment:

the assurance, compliance, readiness, and evidence layer for regulated drone operations.

That segment is smaller than the whole drone software market, but it is more differentiated, more credible for VerityATLAS's current build, and more aligned with the real strengths visible in the FSMS system.

The more the build matures around OA, insurance, personnel scope, and tenant-aware evidence handling, the stronger the case becomes that VerityATLAS should be sold first into serious regulated operators rather than broad low-assurance drone users.
